In our latest session, students tackled building a robot capable of completing multiple tasks autonomously. They learned basic mechanics, motor control, sensor integration, and programming in Python. The project encouraged teamwork, problem-solving, and creative thinking, culminating in a fun mini-competition.

======= There is something oddly intimidating about saying you are interested in engineering when you are still in high school. It sounds serious. It sounds like you are already supposed to know what you are doing. But the truth is, most of us are just figuring it out as we go.

When I first started thinking about engineering, I imagined people building robots, designing bridges, or working on things that felt far out of reach. It seemed like a world meant for people who were naturally brilliant at math and science. I did not immediately see myself fitting into that image. But the more I explored it, the more I realized engineering is not about having everything figured out. It is about being curious enough to keep asking questions!

In high school, engineering often starts in small ways. It might be a physics problem that finally clicks after struggling with it for an hour. It could be coding a simple program and watching it actually work. Sometimes it is joining a club, like robotics or engineering design, and realizing that problem solving can actually be collaborative and even fun. These moments are not dramatic, but they slowly build confidence.

One thing that surprised me is how much engineering overlaps with creativity. It is not just about getting the right answer. It is about finding a way to get there. Two people can approach the same problem completely differently, and both solutions can work. That freedom makes it feel less rigid than I expected, it feels more like building something from scratch rather than following a strict set of rules, and to me–that’s the beauty of engineering.

At the same time, it is not always easy. There are definitely moments where concepts feel confusing or overwhelming. It is easy to compare yourself to others who seem to understand things faster or explain ideas more clearly. But engineering is not a race; passion isn’t a race. It is more like a long process of learning how to think, and struggling with a concept doesn’t mean you aren’t good at it, it usually means you are in the middle of learning something expansive.

Another important part of exploring engineering in high school is realizing how many different paths exist. Engineering is not just one thing. There is mechanical, electrical, biomedical, software, and so many more. In addition to that, there are so many intersections–not directly linked with physically engineering something but helping in the process of it. Others focus on data, algorithms, or design, and others can focus on the marketing, product flows, and distribution. You do not need to decide everything right away. High school is the time to explore and notice what actually interests you, and connect it in a way that feels natural to you.

For me, the most interesting part has been seeing how engineering connects to real life. It is behind the apps we use, the systems we rely on, and even the way businesses make decisions. It is not just theory. It is practical, constantly evolving, and everywhere.

If you are a high school student or middle school student thinking about engineering, you do not need to have a perfect plan. You do not need to be the best in your class or know exactly which field you want to pursue. What matters more is being willing to try, to ask questions, and to keep going even when things feel difficult. Take time to learn, and the rest will come to you with continued effort! >>>>>>> 685c4d8 (Initial commit with updates)
