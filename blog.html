Engineering Your Attitude: Why the Challenge of Engineering Is What Makes It Meaningful


By Ayesha Jain



There is something oddly intimidating about saying you are interested in engineering when you are still in high school. It sounds serious. It sounds like you are already supposed to know what you are doing. But the truth is, most of us are just figuring it out as we go.

When I first started thinking about engineering, I imagined people building robots, designing bridges, or working on things that felt far out of reach. It seemed like a world meant for people who were naturally brilliant at math and science. I did not immediately see myself fitting into that image. But the more I explored it, the more I realized engineering is not about having everything figured out. It is about being curious enough to keep asking questions!

In high school, engineering often starts in small ways. It might be a physics problem that finally clicks after struggling with it for an hour. It could be coding a simple program and watching it actually work. Sometimes it is joining a club, like robotics or engineering design, and realizing that problem solving can actually be collaborative and even fun. These moments are not dramatic, but they slowly build confidence.

One thing that surprised me is how much engineering overlaps with creativity. It is not just about getting the right answer. It is about finding a way to get there. Two people can approach the same problem completely differently, and both solutions can work. That freedom makes it feel less rigid than I expected, it feels more like building something from scratch rather than following a strict set of rules, and to me–that’s the beauty of engineering.

At the same time, it is not always easy. There are definitely moments where concepts feel confusing or overwhelming. It is easy to compare yourself to others who seem to understand things faster or explain ideas more clearly. But engineering is not a race; passion isn’t a race. It is more like a long process of learning how to think, and struggling with a concept doesn’t mean you aren’t good at it, it usually means you are in the middle of learning something expansive.

Another important part of exploring engineering in high school is realizing how many different paths exist. Engineering is not just one thing. There is mechanical, electrical, biomedical, software, and so many more. In addition to that, there are so many intersections–not directly linked with physically engineering something but helping in the process of it. Others focus on data, algorithms, or design, and others can focus on the marketing, product flows, and distribution. You do not need to decide everything right away. High school is the time to explore and notice what actually interests you, and connect it in a way that feels natural to you.

For me, the most interesting part has been seeing how engineering connects to real life. It is behind the apps we use, the systems we rely on, and even the way businesses make decisions. It is not just theory. It is practical, constantly evolving, and everywhere.

If you are a high school student or middle school student thinking about engineering, you do not need to have a perfect plan. You do not need to be the best in your class or know exactly which field you want to pursue. What matters more is being willing to try, to ask questions, and to keep going even when things feel difficult. Take time to learn, and the rest will come to you with continued effort! UpDraft is a great place to start if you want to explore engineering in a supportive environment. We are all learning together, and we can’t wait to see what you create!

It’s Not Rocket Science… Yet.


By Emma James



When people hear the word engineering, they usually think of someone who’s insanely smart.

Someone perfect at math, building robots at age 10, never getting anything wrong.
I used to think that too.

But engineering, at its core, isn’t about being a genius.
It’s not about always being right, either.

Engineering is about curiosity, problem-solving, and teamwork — especially when you’re wrong.

In fact, things aren’t supposed to make sense right away.
Nothing is easy the first time around.

Being wrong is why we do engineering in the first place.
We are always learning, and always improving.

Engineering is about trying.
About asking questions.
About figuring this out through trial and error.

In UpDraft Engineering, you don’t have to struggle alone.

You can:
  1. Ask questions when you’re stuck
  2. Test fun ideas with peers
  3. Turn ideas into something real

Our community is built for:
  1. Building things with your hands
  2. Learning by doing
  3. Meeting people who are figuring it out just like you

You don’t need to know everything to start.
You just need to start.

Airplanes!


By Wolfie Nguyen



When I was young, my father often joked with me that “anything can fly—at least once.” And from a technical standpoint, you really can’t dispute this. The phrase “when pigs fly” is supposed to imply that something is highly unlikely or impossible. But if I shoot a swine out of a cannon off of a cliff, you can’t deny that it flew, at least for a little bit. Just disregard the fact that it never will again. But if you’d like to learn how you can make things that fly more than once, you’re reading the right thing. Here’s why you should consider aerospace engineering and how you can get into it.


Part 1: You won’t be homeless

I knew I wanted to design planes since I was 4. This came with the unfortunate side effect of people saying I might end up kinda poor.
Aeronautical engineering from the outside seems like a very niche field. As I grew up, my friends and family would always ask me, “aren’t the jobs you can apply for so limited?” The answer is no.
And since this nonprofit was founded on the idea of inspiring new engineers, I figured this would be an excellent place to plant the seeds of some future airplane junkies.


Aerospace is often seen as not worth it. It’s commonly cited as one of the most difficult engineering disciplines and is also alleged to have very few job opportunities; essentially it's rumoured to be a path that puts you through hell all to have no money by the end of it.
But don’t worry kids, you’ll get your moolah yet. It is not nearly as limited as people believe it to be. Here are the two main reasons:


  1. Transferability
    If you told me that Leonardo Da Vinci couldn’t draw a 2-dimensional sketch of a flower, I’d probably admit you to a mental hospital.
    This relationship can also be observed in engineering. If you earned a degree that prepared you for designing machines that carry hundreds of people thousands of feet above the ground without killing them, I’m going to assume you have the skills to build something that has far less dire consequences. It’s very common for aerospace engineers to work outside of that sector. You will be fine regardless of the degree you get. I am fully prepared to take on other jobs. While my dream is to work at Boeing on their next airliner, I would be perfectly happy designing vending machines or whatever. They will hire you. You’re not screwed.
    You will be learning the same skills as Mech E’s anyway, just with a few specific classes exclusive to AE. Mechanical and Aerospace engineers are often viewed as interchangeable. Now of course this begs the question, “then why get a specific aerospace degree anyway?”

  2. You’ll get the ladies
    Please disregard the title of this paragraph. If you’re going into any kind of engineering, you probably won’t be getting any ladies. The idea here is just that you may seem more appealing or impressive if your main goal is to work on air and spacecraft. As mentioned before, if you choose to go into aerospace you will be learning largely the same things as a mechanical engineer would. But to really put this into perspective, aerospace is literally viewed as mechanical engineering with a few more classes. I would recommend aerospace engineering if you know you’re going for aerospace jobs just to give you that edge over the mech E’s that may be applying for your same position. But if it doesn’t work out, you can always go for mechanical jobs.


Part 2: How to become a nerd

Now you’re probably wondering how someone would go about applying for aerospace in college. Based on my experience, here are some tips:

  1. START EARLY.
    I cannot stress this enough. I’ve heard of people suddenly deciding they want to go into aerospace late into the stage of deciding on a major. If you’re in middle school right now or even freshman year, try to get started with projects, extracurriculars, or online courses to learn relevant skills. Most people applying to aerospace are people who are absolutely obsessed with the field. If you want to compete with other applicants, I would strongly suggest displaying investment and passion early so you have a lot of source material to back up your application. Don’t wait until junior year. I started early (and I mean really early, I’m talking around 4 years old) and I’m still terrified for my college apps. If you want a fast, good introduction to get yourself a solid understanding for the next few years check out A. Kermode’s Flight Without Formulae. It explains aeronautics without all that yucky math.

  2. Do projects and showcase them
    I personally believe they are the best way of differentiating yourself since pretty much everyone has amazing grades and STEM-based clubs/competitions. I’ve been learning about airplanes for over a decade. My father would always teach me about different models and why certain design choices were made. This led to projects specifically centered around aircraft or aerospace components. My first love was optimizing paper gliders with new designs. Since then, I’ve designed applications for spheroid winglets, wrote training code for a gardening drone, and recently just finished designing an optimized wing configuration for solar aircraft. I will be honest, these projects are carrying my application. Fortunately, many top-tier and highly selective engineering colleges allow or encourage maker portfolio submissions. Even if you don’t have a ton of knowledge on flight design, getting started isn’t super hard, and you will learn a lot more by building things than by reading a textbook or watching a random YouTube video.

  3. LEARN TO WRITE AND GIVE PRESENTATIONS
    It’s a strange thing to emphasize, but make sure you have solid humanities skills on top of your STEM ones. Lab reports, technical overviews of projects, idea pitches etc... You gotta know how to present and sell.

  4. Take AP Stats sophomore year
    This was one of the best decisions I made. It’s very, very useful for research and data explanation. Taking it earlier helps you actually apply the knowledge during high school.

  5. Connect projects to your interests
    This makes things more enjoyable and unique. For example, combine meteorology with aircraft design. For me, it was humorous writing—I wrote a funny aeronautics textbook.

Part 3: I screwed up, please don’t be like me

  1. Not getting ahead in math
    If you’re going into aerospace, you must be strong in math. I recommend taking calculus as early as sophomore year. This allows time for advanced courses like multivariable calculus later.

  2. Overloading junior year
    Doing a NASA program during junior year was very stressful. Balancing it with Calc and Physics made both harder. Consider summer programs instead.

  3. Lack of purpose in projects
    Some projects were just for fun without real application. Try to create 2–3 projects with clear, useful goals. For example: drone automation or aerodynamic optimization.

Part 4: That’s all, folks. Have fun!

So that’s it. I’m still really young and inexperienced, so I didn’t have as much advice as you may have been hoping for. I hope you guys view aerospace differently, and maybe even join me in it. If you need help with projects or just want to chat, feel free to reach me: khanh.nguyen380655@gmail.com Good luck! And make sure to join UpDraft, a great place to kick off your engineering journey :)

How A Single-Board $35 Computer Taught Me A Valuable Lesson In Engineering


By Sonu Rao



Hey there beautiful! Finally, the words I had been searching for appeared on the screen after hours of running commands. Looking back, it seems silly and funny. Alas, let me walk you through how I got here.

I will never forget the sigh of relief, the overheating smell of the Raspberry Pi, connected to a half functioning, cracked, 5-year old computer screen. After agreeing to my dad to spearhead the trending Raspberry Pi project of making a magic mirror, I figured it would be no big deal to set it up. Download the folders, run a few commands on the terminal, and it should work. Except… it didn’t.

Immediately the blame went to my sister – the one who had been messing with the system just a few days ago with no experience. Surely she did something to mess it up. But condemning her would cause me to go down a route with a dead end – not worth it. So, I decided to do some research and watch videos. After running command after command, asking for my dad’s help, 2 hours later there was no solution. Then I found a video with only 928 views. “Last video,” I sighed before yawning, exhausted after staying 1 hour past my bedtime. “Surely this won’t help,” I thought. But it did.

The error was simple: a mismatched Node.js version. No matter how many times I tried to upgrade my Raspberry Pi’s Node.js version to the latest, every time I typed the node –-version command, it stubbornly showed 18.6.3, even after ten rounds of trial and error. But this video unveiled an alternate method: using the node version manager (NVM) to properly switch Node.js versions. That one fix changed everything. One little line that I spent hours obsessing over. The exhilaration of finally solving the problem lifted the heavy weight off my chest and made me so happy that I ran excitedly to my dad, almost damaging the switchboard in the process (lesson learned for the future – that was not a great move).

I’m sharing this experience because a few hours of hard work taught me engineering, any engineering field, is more than solving problems. The process to get there equally matters. Engineers go through hours of just identifying the problem, and many more to solve it. And if you ever come across a problem, don’t panic or be mad at yourself.

At Updraft, we have gone through this process many times. And our projects won’t always go your way. That’s why our talented and experienced team is here to help you, support you, and guide you. As you continue through your engineering journey, remember that every problem has a solution – whether it’s as conspicuous as the sun on a cloudless day or buried deep down in a labyrinth of obstacles, waiting for you to tackle. The problems never stop – but so do the endless solutions.
